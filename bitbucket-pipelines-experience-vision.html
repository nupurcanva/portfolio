Creating an experience vision for Bitbucket Pipelines

Defining strategy and long-term vision for CI/CD management through collaborative workshops and design thinking

In August 2021, I led a workshop with several team members in the Bitbucket team in Sydney to better understand the challenges our customers face with CI/CD management.

üíå Introduction

I worked as a designer on the Bitbucket team for 2 years. During the last few months, we set on a mission to determine the strategy and long-term vision for Bitbucket Pipelines, which is a CI/CD management tool. I did this by conducting a 2-day workshop with various stakeholders on defining the customer problems and defining what an end vision would look like. We prioritized them and explored various themes as described below. Finally, we set some guiding design principles for the experience.

üÜï About this Project

This work was intended to be used to spark conversation on our product strategy, roadmap and future envisioning pieces. The goal of this effort was to align and inspire our teams with an actionable experience vision for Bitbucket that connects the dots between the current strategy and work streams.

The problem

There was no visualization that connected the dots across the multi-year strategy and the vision for Bitbucket. This made it incredibly difficult for teams, stakeholders, and customers to grok what Bitbucket Pipelines is and to get excited about where we're going in the future.

There was an opportunity to do three things:

  • Communicate our strategy in a clear, fun, easily accessible, and memorable way.
  • Inspire and rally the team and wider Atlassian audiences.
  • Excite and inspire our customers and other stakeholders, bringing them on our journey.

üßëüèº‚Äçüíª Setting the context: What are Bitbucket Pipelines (CI/CD)?

Imagine a developer writing some code. They push it to the cloud to integrate it with the rest of the code. Every time they push it, it is being tested, verified & integrated into the codebase and delivered to their customers. This is known as CI/CD (Continuous integration and continuous deployment) and Bitbucket helps them manage code and the CI/CD flow.

üç≤ Problem framing: The story today

üí°
I analyzed 1000+ responses from a customer satisfaction feedback report and interviewed 8 participants to share a list of problems for the workshop.

CI/CD pipelines are mission-critical for businesses. They require scale, concurrency, flexibility, security and infrastructure. With the Bitbucket Carebear initiatives, we expect medium to large enterprise customers to move to Bitbucket and Pipelines.

At a high level, when using the CI/CD capabilities in Bitbucket today, we can see that new customers planning this move will face several key challenges, as well as customers today, have reported similar challenges:

‚öîÔ∏è Missing platform support and key features

The rise in technology has given the teams the flexibility to choose their own infrastructure, language, OS and architecture. As Pipelines does not provide support for popular use cases such as Windows, Buildkit and Mac OS - not to mention features like bring-your-own runners - our customers are left with no option but to choose another competing product.

üçû Work is spread across multiple repositories and is interdependent

Customers need to share code and pipelines between repositories, as many customers have one repository per service and they need to make sure the dependent services can work together. This would need some automation tools between repositories and pipelines.

üíØ Need for visibility and insights

Customers have to monitor deployments across environments and they have different microservices spread across repositories. All of that metadata could be presented with insights to the customers managing different repositories at a time. A key missing feature is the cross-project and cross repository view of their deployment status that reduces manually checking for updates and helps teams make release decisions based on insights.

üìè Scalability for large teams

Quarterly reports have shown that the adoption of Bitbucket Pipelines is reducing in larger teams and we believe the main cause of the churn is scalability limits. With Carebear, many mid-market and enterprise teams will be moving to Bitbucket, and we need to ensure Bitbucket Pipelines is ready for them.

üåà Defining the design principles

What are Design Principles and why use them?

Design principles are a set of considerations that form the basis of any good product. They are empirical, experience-backed, strongly-held opinions about the experience we want to create. An entire team agrees to follow them when designing products and services. They are directive rather than prescriptive; in other words, they speak to the what and why of our designs, not the how.

Design principles help teams with decision-making. When it comes to making tradeoffs or hard choices, first principles guide us to still be true to our sense of purpose and find clarity amidst the noise.

üíô What about Bitbucket Principles?

How might we come out with principles that champion the users we are designing for, yet still match the purpose and feel familiar with Atlassian principles/values? Given the situation outlined in the section above, we've outlined the key themes we believe are critical to the future of CI/CD and will help us create more meaningful experiences in the sections below.

üîß Flexible with customer's software

People's working styles and the technologies they use will be more flexible in the future. Choosing a different technology or adapting software's experiences' flexibility helps our products serve our customers from concept to completion.

üìä Enriched and actionable

We provide actionable insights for teams to make informed decisions. The teams do not rely on senior engineers to push a manual button but feel confident to take action.

üéì Guide to mastery

Large teams will rely on pipelines to achieve more, and we can help large teams move quicker if they have visibility, more control of users and fewer limitations from the product.

ü§ù Collaboration over silos

By knowing the developers who use our CI/CD platform, we can provide collaboration. It should be more immersive and participatory than ever before, with more human interaction and meaning. Utilize Atlassian's advantage to provide rich collaboration.

ü´Ç Customer Profiles

We have two kinds of users - the End users and the orchestrators. They may be a part of the same team or different teams, based on the team structure. In most experiences, the end-users make use of the system set up by the orchestrators.

üîë Journey key points

üî≠ Where we're going

For CI and CD, we're considering a multi-year strategy. The next few items are our vision for the next 18 months. This is our north star vision, not a list of road mapped items. The goal is to motivate people to think about what they can do in the future. Here are a few examples of how we believe our product can add value and solve customer pain points.

Build and Test metrics

üìä
Enriched

Provide contextual build metrics like build times, test results history, flaky tests, poor tests, build time breakdown, build prediction and different step statuses to improve the build and test experience and increase build speed.

Automated deployment checks

üìä
Enriched

Allow for Automated decision making, Automatically rollback if the deployment is unhealthy or stopped and Visual history over time with links to PRs/incidents.

Services in Bitbucket

üìä
Enriched

Making Services in Bitbucket First-class citizens would allow us to show if an alert is ongoing for a service (pull from opsgenie).

Drag and drop pipeline builder

ü§ù
Collaborative

Allow users to collaborate and build a pipeline configuration from various points in Bitbucket, while they can see a live preview and test the builds before running and share code with the team.

Jira Integration for Code insights

üìä
Enriched

Make insights more actionable and trackable by allowing users to create action items for security, code quality and coverage in Jira with pre-filled data and remediation.

Artifact management in Bitbucket

üìä
Enriched & Flexible

Integrate with artifact management tools to provide insights such as artifact health or usage within bitbucket. Increase limits for artifacts for larger teams and allow them to browse/manage artifacts in Bitbucket.

Support multiple platforms and tools

üîß
Flexible

Add support for teams that build using windows, macOS and ARM. Support tools like Docker compose and Buildkit. This can be a friction point for large teams and prevent them from adopting Bitbucket pipelines.

Lock deployments / Comment on deployments

ü§ù
Collaborative

Allow teams to set deployment times and locking in case of incidents and off timings, comment on deployments if they are unhealthy or failed for better visibility.

Multi-step deployments

üìä
Enriched & Flexible

Allowing grouping of deployments. It is needed for workflows that have branches that are environment based - dev branch for dev deployments, staging for staging and prod for prod etc. We also don't support branch-specific variables, which means users look to deployment-specific variables.

Deployments and monitoring dashboard

üìä
Enriched

Showing deployment history, historic durations of deployments, Link deployments to SignalFx, Display health status of environments and Show when deployments happened in SignalFx will help visualize the decision to deploy and monitor post-deployment statistics.

ü§î How might we fail

We did this activity at the end of ideation. I personally love this activity. It's a real insight into what our users see as success and what they see as a failure in our delivery of a solution. It gives our users the opportunity to tell us what we need to consider when designing a solution to their problems. It also allows us back in the office to set success criteria and acceptance targets to ensure that the solution we design meets the expectations of our users.
