When I joined Canva's Ecosystem team as Lead Product Designer, we were making a fundamental mistake: treating all developers as a monolithic group. Our team would refer to "developers" as if they all had the same needs, goals, and pain points. The reality was far more complex.

Canva's developer ecosystem had grown to include everyone from independent hobbyists building weekend projects to enterprise teams creating mission-critical integrations. Yet our product decisions were largely based on internal developer opinions rather than real user research.

üîç Understanding the Problem Space

The Current State of Developer Experience

Our developers were struggling with multiple pain points across their journey. Through extensive research, I identified three critical areas of friction:

App Review Process

Feedback was valuable but often too slow, leading to delays. Canva requirements around copyright and UI often felt excessive and demotivating.

Authentication Challenges

Integrating with Canva's backend systems was difficult and time-consuming, creating dependencies that delayed releases.

Monetization Barriers

Developers had to direct users to separate sites for payment, causing major drop-off with no integrated monetization path.

The Research Journey

To truly understand our developers, I synthesized insights from multiple sources:

  • User research: Extensive interviews and usability sessions with developers
  • Usage data: Analysis showing which apps were succeeding and failing
  • Support insights: Examination of tickets and forum discussions for recurring pain points

Key Discovery: Developers weren't a single group but rather distinct behavioral clusters with different needs, motivations, and ways of working.

üèóÔ∏è Creating the Archetype Framework

Why Archetypes, Not Personas?

Traditional personas focus on demographics: job titles, age, location, even fake photos. But as we noted in our planning documents:

üí°
"While these are useful for some teams like Marketing, they can lead to unconscious stereotyping. Because so many different types of people use Canva, it's often more useful for product teams to consider their similarities outside of demographics."

Archetypes transcend roles and focus on behavior, goals, motivations, and pain points. A marketer, an office manager, and an IT intern might all exhibit the same behavioral patterns when building with Canva.

The Three Dimensions of Developer Behavior

Through our research, I identified three key dimensions that defined how developers interact with Canva:

Support Needed

From completely self-sufficient developers to those requiring high-touch support with frequent meetings

Flexibility with Design

From expecting Canva to adapt to their designs to prioritizing Canva's design guidelines above all

Speed of Releasing

From expecting apps to go live in under a week to accepting months-long review processes

üé® Creating the Archetypes: My Design Process

To create the developer archetypes, I began by synthesizing extensive research from over 40 developer interviews and usability sessions. Rather than simply categorizing developers by job titles, I identified behavioral patterns that transcended roles. Through careful analysis, I discovered three critical dimensions that defined developer behavior: level of support needed, flexibility with design standards, and expected speed to release.

I led cross-functional workshops with product managers, engineers, and designers to validate these patterns. Using FigJam, I facilitated ideation sessions where we mapped developer behaviors to their underlying needs and motivations. This collaborative approach ensured the archetypes would be actionable across all teams.

From this synthesis, I defined five distinct archetypes: Optimizers, Go-Getters, Platform Amplifiers, System Integrators, and Enterprise Customizers. For each archetype, I documented their values, pain points, gains, and linked them to real apps in our ecosystem as concrete examples.

Implementation Toolkit Created:

  • Figma components with archetype cards for design reviews
  • Dovetail tags for research categorization
  • Updated PRD templates with archetype fields
  • Visual archetype cards showing support needs, design flexibility, and release speed expectations on clear scales

I validated the framework by immediately applying it to active projects, including the Developer Experience Vision and RICE platform planning. By analyzing our metrics through the archetype lens, I revealed why certain features had low enterprise adoption but high usage among independent developers.

The result: A behavioral framework that transformed how Canva understands its developer community, moving from assumptions to data-driven insights that teams could act on immediately.

üë• The Five Developer Archetypes

1. Optimizers: The Independent Innovators

Who they are: Independent developers and Canva enthusiasts who use APIs to enhance Canva's functionality, solve niche problems, and explore monetization potential. They might be freelance developers, side-project enthusiasts, or "no-code" tool users.

What they value:

  • Good documentation and resources with clearly documented APIs, SDKs, and processes
  • Comprehensive usage examples and tutorials
  • Guidance on monetization and business potential for apps

Their gains: Adding value to Canva gives them a sense of achievement and community contribution. Working with Canva's APIs allows them to learn new skills and stay up to date with trends while opening opportunities to earn money.

Their pains: Difficult to find direct contact information for support, limited access to business-related information, multiple rounds of review with inconsistent feedback, and lack of access to early adopters for testing.

2. Go-Getters: The Fast-Moving Innovators

Who they are: Developers who prioritize speed to market and view Canva as a launching pad for their tool or product. Often founders, CTOs, software engineers, or solopreneurs who wear multiple hats.

‚ö°
Key insight: Go-Getters often have many things happening simultaneously, so lengthy review processes lead them to lose interest and focus on other platforms.

3. Platform Amplifiers: The Strategic Integrators

Who they are: Teams working to integrate Canva into their own established product or strategically add their product to Canva's UI. They enhance workflows for their existing audience while leveraging Canva's design ecosystem.

4. System Integrators: The Enterprise Builders

Who they are: Agencies or developers working to seamlessly weave Canva into workflows, enhancing user experiences and driving adoption. They often have direct relationships with Canva, collaborating on marquee apps and integrations.

5. Enterprise Customizers: The Workflow Optimizers

Who they are: Workflow optimizers who work for companies wanting to privately introduce Canva into their existing workflow, making it easier for employees to stay on brand and increase efficiency.

Critical insight: Each individual can wear many "hats." A developer might be an Optimizer when building a side project but act as a System Integrator when working on a client project.

üíº Real-World Application

The archetypes immediately proved their value in several key projects:

Developer Experience Vision

We used archetypes to shape our 2-year roadmap, identifying which user segments our business strategy should prioritize for maximum impact. Each archetype's pain points directly informed our strategic initiatives.

RICE Platform Development

Understanding what different archetypes were building helped us prioritize platform services. We mapped out what Optimizers needed (frontend-focused tools) versus System Integrators (authentication gateways, secrets management, queuing systems).

Review Process Optimization

Key insight: By understanding that Go-Getters often have many things happening simultaneously, we recognized that lengthy review processes would lead them to lose interest and focus on other platforms. This insight drove our push for streamlined, archetype-appropriate review cycles.

üìä Measuring Impact

The Metrics That Mattered

We tracked adoption and engagement across different archetypes. The data revealed interesting patterns:

  • Non-restricted apps significantly outperformed restricted ones: 37,592 MAUs compared to just 203 for restricted apps
  • Growing adoption: Steady increases in total MAUs for Connect Integrations, peaking at 327,616
  • Opportunity identified: Even popular apps like Mockups had low percentage adoption within subscription tiers
üíé
"Even 20 people using DAU/MAU is super value! But they are using Canva because of that integration! Value is in frequency and usage, not number of users."

Critical Insights from Implementation

The archetypes revealed that different developer types have vastly different ROI expectations. Optimizers often treat their apps as hobby or passion projects rather than serious business ventures‚Äîthey're glad to get ROI but it's not their primary motivation.

üîÆ The Future: AI Agents as a New Archetype

As we refined our understanding of developer archetypes, we began exploring a potential sixth archetype: AI Agents. These would be AI systems designed to autonomously develop, deploy, and manage apps within Canva.

Forward-thinking insight: "AI and LLMs speak the language of structured data. We need documentation that can be parsed by AI, to understand which APIs to call for what job."

The emergence of tools like ChatGPT and Claude that can interact with Canva opened new possibilities. This forward-thinking approach positioned us to embrace AI agents as part of our ecosystem strategy, ensuring Canva would be ready for the next evolution in developer tools.

üí≠ Reflections and Learnings

The Power of Behavioral Understanding

The most significant outcome of this project wasn't just the archetypes themselves, but the fundamental shift in how Canva thinks about developers. We moved from making assumptions based on internal opinions to making decisions grounded in real user research and behavioral patterns.

The Challenge of Balance

One of our ongoing challenges was ensuring we didn't over-index on any single archetype. While it might be tempting to focus on the "biggest fish," we recognized that a healthy ecosystem needs diversity. Optimizers might build the innovative features that attract users, while System Integrators create the robust integrations that retain enterprise customers.

The Importance of Evolution

üîÑ
Living framework: Archetypes aren't static. We built in mechanisms for continuous validation and refinement as the ecosystem evolves and new technologies emerge.
